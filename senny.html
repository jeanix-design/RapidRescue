🩹 First Aid Guidance

Quick First Aid Reference

⚠️ Important: These guidelines are for emergency situations only. Always call professional emergency services for serious injuries or illnesses.

All
🔴 Critical
🤕 Injuries
🏥 Medical
🌡️ Environmental

CPR (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation)

For someone who is unresponsive and not breathing normally.

1 Check for responsiveness: Tap and shout "Are you okay?"
2 Call emergency services immediately or ask someone to call
3 Place person on their back on a firm surface
4 Place heel of one hand on center of chest, other hand on top
5 Push hard and fast: 100-120 compressions per minute, 2 inches deep
6 Allow chest to fully recoil between compressions
7 Continue until help arrives or person shows signs of life
Only perform CPR if trained. If untrained, do hands-only CPR (compressions only).

🚨 Call Emergency Services Now

📞 911 (US) | 112 (EU) | 999 (UK) | 110/119 (Japan)

Choking (Heimlich Maneuver)

For someone who cannot breathe, speak, or cough due to blocked airway.

1 Ask: "Are you choking?" If they nod yes and can't speak, act immediately
2 Stand behind person, wrap arms around their waist
3 Make a fist with one hand, place thumb side against abdomen above navel
4 Grasp fist with other hand, give quick upward thrusts
5 Continue until object is dislodged or person becomes unconscious
6 If person becomes unconscious, begin CPR and call emergency services
For pregnant or obese persons: Place hands on chest instead of abdomen.

Severe Bleeding

For heavy bleeding that won't stop with normal pressure.

1 Call emergency services immediately
2 Have person lie down and keep calm
3 Apply direct pressure with clean cloth or gauze
4 If blood soaks through, add more layers - DO NOT remove original dressing
5 If possible, elevate injured area above heart level
6 If bleeding doesn't stop and is life-threatening, apply tourniquet 2-3 inches above wound
7 Note time tourniquet was applied and tell emergency responders
Never remove embedded objects. Apply pressure around the object, not on it.

Burns

For thermal, chemical, or electrical burns.

1 Stop the burning process: Remove from heat source
2 Cool the burn: Run cool (not cold) water over burn for 10-20 minutes
3 Remove jewelry or tight clothing near burn before swelling starts
4 Cover with sterile, non-stick bandage or clean cloth
5 Do NOT break blisters, apply ice, butter, or ointments
6 For chemical burns: Flush with water for 20+ minutes, remove contaminated clothing
7 Seek medical attention for: large burns, face/hands/feet/genitals burns, or signs of infection

Fractures & Sprains

For suspected broken bones or joint injuries.

1 Keep person still and calm; do NOT move injured area
2 Call emergency services if: deformity, severe pain, inability to move, or injury to head/neck/back
3 Apply ice pack wrapped in cloth to reduce swelling (20 min on, 20 min off)
4 If trained and necessary, immobilize with splint (use rigid object + bandages)
5 Elevate injured area if possible to reduce swelling
6 Monitor for signs of shock: pale skin, rapid breathing, weakness
Never try to straighten a deformed limb. Immobilize in position found.

Shock

For person showing signs of shock: pale/clammy skin, rapid weak pulse, confusion, fainting.

1 Call emergency services immediately
2 Have person lie down on their back
3 Elevate legs 8-12 inches (unless head/neck/back/leg injuries suspected)
4 Keep person warm with blanket (not hot)
5 Loosen tight clothing
6 Do NOT give food or drink
7 Monitor breathing and be ready to perform CPR if needed
Shock is life-threatening. Always seek immediate medical attention.

Severe Allergic Reaction (Anaphylaxis)

For symptoms: difficulty breathing, swelling of face/throat, hives, rapid pulse, dizziness.

1 Call emergency services immediately
2 Ask if person has epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen)
3 If yes, help them use it: Remove safety cap, press firmly against outer thigh, hold for 3 seconds
4 Have person lie down with legs elevated (unless breathing is easier sitting up)
5 Loosen tight clothing, cover with blanket
6 Do NOT give oral medications if person is having trouble breathing
7 If no improvement in 5-15 minutes and second auto-injector available, administer second dose
Anaphylaxis can be fatal. Even if symptoms improve, person MUST go to hospital.

Heart Attack Symptoms

For symptoms: chest pain/pressure, pain in arm/jaw/back, shortness of breath, nausea, cold sweat.

1 Call emergency services IMMEDIATELY - do not wait
2 Have person stop all activity and rest in comfortable position (usually sitting)
3 Loosen tight clothing
4 Ask if person takes heart medication (nitroglycerin) and help them take it if prescribed
5 If person is not allergic and has no bleeding disorder, give 1 adult aspirin (325mg) to chew
6 Monitor breathing and be ready to perform CPR if person becomes unresponsive
7 Stay with person until help arrives
Time is critical. Every minute counts - call emergency services immediately.

Stroke (Remember FAST)

For symptoms: sudden numbness, confusion, trouble speaking, vision problems, severe headache.

1 Face: Ask person to smile. Does one side droop?
2 Arms: Ask person to raise both arms. Does one drift downward?
3 Speech: Ask person to repeat simple phrase. Is speech slurred or strange?
4 Time: If ANY of these signs, call emergency services IMMEDIATELY
5 Note time symptoms first appeared - critical for treatment
6 Have person lie down with head slightly elevated
7 Do NOT give food, drink, or medications
8 Monitor breathing and be ready for CPR
"Time lost is brain lost." Stroke treatment is most effective within 3-4.5 hours of symptom onset.

Poisoning

For suspected ingestion, inhalation, or skin contact with poison.

1 Call Poison Control immediately: 📞 1-800-222-1222 (US) or local emergency number
2 If person is unconscious, not breathing, or having seizures: Call emergency services immediately
3 For swallowed poison: Do NOT induce vomiting unless instructed by Poison Control
4 For inhaled poison: Move person to fresh air immediately
5 For skin contact: Remove contaminated clothing, rinse skin with water for 15-20 minutes
6 For eye contact: Rinse eyes with lukewarm water for 15-20 minutes, holding eyelids open
7 Have container or substance information ready for Poison Control
Never give anything by mouth to an unconscious person.

Seizures

For someone having a seizure (convulsions, loss of consciousness).

1 Stay calm and note time seizure starts
2 Keep person safe: Clear area of hard/sharp objects, place soft item under head
3 Turn person onto their side (recovery position) to keep airway clear
4 Loosen tight clothing around neck
5 Do NOT restrain person or put anything in their mouth
6 Stay with person until seizure ends and they are fully conscious
7 Call emergency services if: seizure lasts >5 minutes, person has repeated seizures, is injured, pregnant, or has diabetes, or if it's their first seizure
Never put anything in the mouth of a person having a seizure. They cannot swallow their tongue.

Heat Exhaustion / Heat Stroke

For symptoms: heavy sweating, weakness, dizziness, nausea, headache, high body temperature.

1 Move person to cool, shaded area immediately
2 Have person lie down and elevate legs slightly
3 Remove excess or tight clothing
4 Cool person: Apply cool wet cloths to head/neck/armpits/groin, fan air over them
5 If conscious and not nauseated, give small sips of cool water or sports drink
6 Monitor for signs of heat stroke: confusion, loss of consciousness, hot/dry skin, body temp >103°F (39.4°C)
7 If heat stroke suspected: Call emergency services immediately, continue cooling measures
Heat stroke is a medical emergency. Can cause organ damage or death if not treated immediately.

Hypothermia

For symptoms: shivering, confusion, slurred speech, drowsiness, weak pulse, slow breathing.

1 Move person to warm, dry place immediately
2 Remove wet clothing, replace with dry layers or blankets
3 Cover head and neck to prevent heat loss
4 If conscious and able to swallow, give warm (not hot) non-alcoholic, non-caffeinated drinks
5 Use skin-to-skin contact under dry blankets if needed
6 Do NOT rub or massage cold limbs - can cause dangerous heart rhythms
7 Handle person gently - rough movement can trigger cardiac arrest in severe hypothermia
8 Call emergency services for moderate to severe hypothermia
Do NOT use direct heat (hot water, heating pads) - can cause burns or dangerous blood flow changes.

Basic Wound Care

For minor cuts, scrapes, and abrasions.

1 Wash hands with soap and water or use hand sanitizer
2 Stop bleeding: Apply gentle pressure with clean cloth or gauze
3 Clean wound: Rinse with clean, running water. Use mild soap around (not in) wound
4 Remove debris: Use tweezers cleaned with alcohol to remove visible dirt
5 Apply antibiotic ointment if available and no allergy
6 Cover with sterile bandage or adhesive strip
7 Change dressing daily or when wet/dirty
8 Watch for infection: increased redness, swelling, warmth, pus, or fever
Seek medical care for: Deep wounds, animal/human bites, puncture wounds, or signs of infection.

When to Call Emergency Services

Call immediately for any of these situations.

✓ Unconsciousness or unresponsiveness
✓ Difficulty breathing or not breathing
✓ Chest pain or pressure lasting more than a few minutes
✓ Severe bleeding that won't stop
✓ Signs of stroke (FAST: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty)
✓ Severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis)
✓ Head, neck, or back injury with pain or numbness
✓ Suspected broken bone with deformity or inability to move
✓ Severe burns (large area, face/hands/feet/genitals, or deep burns)
✓ Poisoning or suspected overdose
✓ Seizure lasting more than 5 minutes or repeated seizures
✓ Suicidal thoughts or self-harm
